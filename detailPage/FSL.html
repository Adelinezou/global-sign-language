Filipino Sign Language (FSL)

Filipino Sign Language (FSL) is the sign language used by the Deaf community in the Philippines. It is a fully developed language with its own grammar and vocabulary, separate from spoken Filipino.

History and Origin

FSL has developed over time, influenced by various regional sign languages and local gestures. It gained recognition as the national sign language of the Philippines through the implementation of the National Council on Disability Affairs (NCDA) and the establishment of the FSL Recognition Act in 2009.

Usage and Community

FSL is used by Deaf individuals, their families, and interpreters in various settings. It serves as an essential means of communication, promoting awareness of Deaf culture and the rights of Deaf individuals in society.

Distinct Features of FSL

  • FSL is a visual language that relies on hand signs, facial expressions, and body movements.
  • The grammar of FSL is different from spoken Filipino, employing its own syntax and structure.
  • Facial expressions and body language are crucial for conveying tone, emotion, and grammatical context.
  • Fingerspelling is utilized for spelling names and specific terms not represented by established signs.

FSL in Education

FSL is increasingly being integrated into educational programs for Deaf students in the Philippines, ensuring that Deaf learners can thrive in academic settings while using their native language.
