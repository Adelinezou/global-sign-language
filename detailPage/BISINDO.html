Indonesian Sign Language (BISINDO)

Indonesian Sign Language (BISINDO) is the sign language used by the Deaf community in Indonesia. It is a natural language that has its own grammar, syntax, and vocabulary, distinct from spoken Indonesian and other sign languages.

History and Origin

BISINDO emerged in the late 20th century as a result of the efforts of Deaf educators and community members who sought to establish a standardized form of sign language. It has evolved over time, influenced by local dialects and other sign languages, to better serve the diverse needs of the Indonesian Deaf community.

Usage and Community

BISINDO is widely used by Deaf individuals in Indonesia, as well as their families, friends, and interpreters. It plays a significant role in the cultural identity of the Deaf community, facilitating communication and social interaction among members.

Distinct Features of BISINDO

  • BISINDO is a visual language that relies on hand shapes, facial expressions, and body movements to convey meaning.
  • It has its own grammatical structure that differs from spoken Indonesian. For example, the order of signs can vary based on context.
  • Facial expressions are crucial in BISINDO for conveying tone, emotions, and grammatical features such as questions or negations.
  • BISINDO includes fingerspelling for spelling out names and terms that do not have a dedicated sign.

BISINDO in Education

BISINDO is taught in schools for the Deaf across Indonesia, and many educational institutions are working to incorporate BISINDO into their curricula to support Deaf students and enhance their learning experience.
