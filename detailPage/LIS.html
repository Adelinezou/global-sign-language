Italian Sign Language (LIS)

Italian Sign Language (LIS) is the sign language used by the Deaf community in Italy. It is a fully developed natural language with its own grammar and vocabulary, distinct from spoken Italian.

History and Origin

The origins of LIS date back to the early 19th century, with influences from various regional sign languages and gestures used across Italy. In 1880, the Milan Conference on Deaf Education had a significant impact on the use of sign languages in Italy, leading to the widespread adoption of LIS in educational institutions for the Deaf.

Usage and Community

LIS is primarily used by Deaf individuals in Italy, along with their families, friends, and interpreters. The Deaf community in Italy values LIS as a key component of their cultural identity, and its use fosters strong connections within the community.

Distinct Features of LIS

  • LIS is a visual language that employs hand shapes, facial expressions, and body movements to convey meaning.
  • It has its own grammatical structure, which differs from spoken Italian, often employing a "topic-comment" order.
  • Facial expressions play an essential role in conveying emotions and grammatical features, such as questions or negations.
  • LIS includes fingerspelling for spelling out names, places, or terms that lack specific signs.

LIS in Education

LIS is taught in schools for the Deaf throughout Italy and is increasingly recognized in mainstream educational contexts. Many Deaf education programs incorporate LIS to facilitate learning and enhance communication.
